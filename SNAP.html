Introduction to SNAP (demonstarted with a number of projects)

This report demonstrates the use of remotely sensed images both radar and optical being Sentinel 1 and Sentinel 2 respectively in analyzing Normalized Difference Moisture Index (NDMI) and multi-temporal analysis. NDMI has many applications including crop water stress monitoring. The preprocessing and analysis tasks undertaken in this report were done with Sentinel Application Platform (SNAP) software and supported with QGIS for creating maps of the results. Images were acquired from Copernicus Open Access Hub. The report is structured to focus on capturing important metadata characteristics, workflow which includes image acquisition, image preprocessing, analysis, and presenting map output as well as interpreting them. For the purpose of clarity and ease of understanding, the general workflow is summarized in a diagram whiles details of the procedure is segmented to reflect the task being undertaken and documented a such. These are aimed at achieving the following main objectives (i) To calculate Normalized Difference Moisture index (NDMI) (ii) To estimate the change of NDMI (change detection) (iii) To create a SAR multi-temporal composite

Preprocessing of Sentinel 2 image of study area

With image processing level of 2A implying image was downloaded already atmospherically corrected, atmospheric correction process was skipped and cloud masking undertaken to remove clouds on the image. Clouds masking involves ‘replacing the reported reflectance values in clouded or shadowed areas of the images with Not Available (NA) values’ (Masiliūnas, 2017). This is aimed at reducing the noise impact of clouds on the image. Resampling was also undertaken to resize the pixels into the same pixel size which is 10 meters (m) spatial resolution. This was done because NDMI uses the Near Infrared (NIR) band which is band 8 with a spatial resolution of 10m which is different from the Short Wave Infrared (SWIR) which is band 11 with a spatial resolution of 60m.

Analysis of image (NDMI and change detection)

The NDMI (Normalized Difference Moisture Index) is computed as the comparison of difference between the NIR and SWIR, and the sum of NIR and SWIR.

Mathematically expressed as;

Typically, NDMI values ranges from -1 to 1 with higher positive values indicating higher moisture content of vegetation and lower positive values indicating vegetation with water stress. Negative values of NDMI indicates non-vegetated land cover. NDMI is also used to explain land cover change overtime hence change detection. Change detection in this report involved recognizing a variation in NDMI values of the study area during different times. This was calculated to reflect the impact of a change in prevailing conditions such as weather on land surface characteristics. The characteristics of land as depicted by an image is best explain by the NDMI range of values for which a threshold is set to delineate regions of high moisture in vegetation. Change detection in NDMI was focused on the regions of high moisture vegetation to map changes in moisture level resulting from seasonal changes. Interpretation NDMI result (2018-04-20)

Depicted in the map is the spatial distribution of NDMI values in the study area. The result is a wide range NDMI value indicating both vegetated and non-vegetated land cover. Basically, using the compass direction illustrated on the map, NDMI values within the range of -0.678 to -0.118 occurred in greater proportion in the far west region of the study area as compared to other regions. Thus, the western region of the study area is made of land cover with barely any vegetation and water stressed. Higher NDMI values (0.428 to 0.913) indicating vegetation with relatively high moisture content was more prevalent in central region of the area as opposed to NDMI values of -0.118 to 0.128 in the extreme east of the study area. In general, the study area is largely covered by surfaces with NDMI values between 0.128 to 0.428 indicative of some vegetative cover with relatively low water content. The interpretation of NDMI is deduced from data provided by Antognelli (2019) which is captured in the appendix. The segment of the image with white colour visualization are masked out clouds which are replaced with ‘No data’ (NaN)

Procedure for estimating NDMI change

To estimate the change of NDMI, vegetative index differencing method was used for high moisture vegetation areas which is delineated with a threshold of 0.5. This method involved the subtraction of NDMI of high moisture vegetation image for 2018-04-20 from NDMI of high moisture vegetation for 2018-10-12 to detect changes in NDMI. Thus, all changes to be discuss and indicated are occurring over areas of high vegetation cover. To undertake such analysis, collocation is done to combine both NDMI bands together in a common folder. Collocation was undertaken by clicking on Raster tool on the menu bar , then navigating to Geometic operations and clicking on Collocate. In the Collocation box, the first preprocessed image which is dated 2018-04-20 was set as the master image and the second preprocessed image set as the slave image in a procedure visualized in the workflow below.

Interpretation of NDMI change

Analyzing the changes in NDMI between the period of April and October for high moisture vegetation, the minimum NDMI change was -0.399 (0.4) and maximum was 0.284 (0.3) as computed from statistics. It can be interpreted that negative NDMI are areas where vegetation lost it water content or increase water stress level and positive NDMI being net gain in moisture level by the vegetation. Thus, the highest lost of moisture by vegetation occurred at areas with -0.4 NDMI change and the highest gain in moisture by vegetation occurred in areas with 0.3 NDMI change. With such a short timelaspe between images and relatively limited range of change, the proposed most plausible cause of change in NDMI is seasonal changes where April image is under the Spring weather condition which changes to Autumn for the October image. The spatial distribution of changes in NDMI is such that moderate loss in moisture (-0.2 to 0.0) occurs more extensively in the south western part of the study area and much lesser in the south eastern part. Further, greater area of vegetation in the western part of the central belt of the study area had an increase in its moisture content (0 to 0.3 NDMI change). Differences in the amount of moisture lost or gain is likely to be contributed by the varying forest and vegetation on the landscape ranging from broad-leaved forest, coniferous forest to mixed forest as verified from 2018 CORINE land cover.

Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) multitemporal composite

Multitemporal composite analysis involves combining images of different timestep to detect changes evident in a study area over time period under study. This analysis is quite simple and efficient in depicting both unchanged and change areas of phenomena being studied. This analysis is aimed at achieving the third objective set in this report.

Image acquisition of Sentinel 1 images

Radar images from Sentinel 1 were used for this type of analysis. Three images of different time steps of the same study area which was selected to be the region of Mekong Delta were used. These images not only reflects different conditions of the study area at different times but also different land use activities in the area. The selection of dates for the image was based on the supposed periods for land preparation, planting and harvesting of rice in certain towns such as Mua. Nonetheless, it is worth noting that different areas undertake different activities at different times. The main phenomena that is being researched with multitemporal composite analysis is land use changes depicting the growth stage of rice cultivation in the Mekong Delta region. To undertake a multitemporal composite, a radar sensed image being sentinel 1 Ground Range Detector in Interferometric Wide (IW) swath mode was used. Three timesteps being 13 th March 2018, 16 th August 2018 and 7 th January 2019 image covering the Mekong delta were download from Copernicus Access Hub. The first timestep image of 13 th March 2018 is displayed in figure 2.6 which is viewed in the Amplitude band in VH polarization. Sentinel 1 images requires preprocessing analysis which are different from optical images of Sentinel 2 which are describe below.

Preprocessing analysis of Sentinel 1 images

Preprocessing of the image began with image calibration which was done to convert pixel values of image to correspond with backscatter of the surface. For this exercise, the importance of calibration lies in comparing images of different times. Also, Speckle filtering was undertaken to improve image interpretation and visualization. Speckle is a “salt and pepper” effect inherent in radar images (Held et al, 2003). Further, terrain correction was undertaken to correct geometric distortions. These preprocessing analysis were repeated for all images of different timesteps. Prior to Multitemporal composite analysis of image, the preprocessed images were converted to decibels in logarithmic scale to improve visualization and stacked together.

Analysis of Sentinel image 1

Multitemporal composite analysis was applied on the preprocessed images by combining the images in RGB band channels to detect changes that have occurred during the time periods analyzed. This produced a multitude of different colours indicating diverse changes mapped. With the aim of only detecting changes in land use with this methodology, the result was not further classified but interpreted to reveal the spatial distribution of the changes. Interpretation of Result

The multitemporal colour composite of images covering the time periods of 13 th March 2018, 16th April 2018 and 7th January 2019 created depicts changes in the backscattering coefficients of land cover and land use such as rice cultivation in the region of Mekong delta. The time-steps indicates the rice growing seasons including harvesting which varies across the Mekong delta. Corresponding to the chosen dates, Mua which is a rice growing community in Mekong undertakes planting in August and harvesting in January (Liew et al, 1997). The map of multitemporal composite illustrates great variation in colour which corresponds to the large variety of rice cropping system, different growing season and variety of rice planted in the Mekong delta region as argued by Xuan (1975) and Liew et al, (1997). The grey areas indicate unchanged areas with relatively constant backscattering coefficient over the time covered. These areas are expected to be non-rice growing areas with relatively more permanent land cover during the timesteps considered. Such areas include inland melaleuca forests, the coastal mangrove forests where there is no significant change in the land cover compared to rice cultivation fields that have changed with farming activities within the short period studied. Grey areas which appear to be linear are linear settlements and roads which had relatively constant backscattering during the period (Liew et al, 1997).
