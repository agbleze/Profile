Introduction to Google Earth Engine Analysis

The era of big data calls for the use of tools with capacity to analyze large volumes of data at fast pace as well as gain deeper understanding of long term spatio-temporal phenomena. Arguably, big data analysis requires capacities that lies beyond the scope of most conventional remote sensing software. This page in demonstrating the practice of techniques in analyzing big data, uses Google Earth Engine for a plethora of geospatial analysis including Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) estimation, binary classification and time series analysis among others. Google Earth Engine (GEE) is a cloud computing platform for geospatial analysis of global coverage. GEE allows the use of both Graphic User Interface (GUI) and Coding User Interface for analysis. The Coding User Interface supports Python and Java-script for coding and an interface for visualization. For this exercise, Java-script was used in the Coding User Interface for creating processing chain for analyzing NDVI Landsat 8 imagery, Random Forest classification of water and no water regions and inspecting time series over different biomes.Dataset used varied from different sensors to include Landsat 8, Sentinel-2, and MODIS. The workflow is illustrated graphically for the purpose of clarity. Insights are also provided on the rational behind the various techniques used and supplemented with links to codes. Further, the results of the various analysis are captured and interpreted accordingly. A great utility in the use of Google Earth Engine and R programming (see section for tutorial) which is explored in these exercises are their ability to automate geospatial processes for a large volume of data with high velocity.

Times series analysis of NDVI for different biomes or vegetation in Google Earth Engine (GEE)

Time series data is one of the big data that we have deal with in recent times. With several satellites operating in space, the volume of data available to us recently is really huge. It only means one thing, we have more to mine to gain insights. One of the ways to draw such insight is to analyse changes over a long time period. With vegetation being one of the major land surface features studied with remote sensing, we will doing a good job by subjecting it to time series analysis. This is what this tutorial seek to achieve, - to analyze time series of NDVI of vegetation or biome over a decade. By the end of this tutorial, we will acquire the skills of

  • Preprocessing of MODIS image for time series analysis
  • Plotting of time series in GEE

Explanation of code

MODIS dataset with 16 days frequency and 250 meters spatial resolution is used for the time series analysis. The MODIS collection with an ID being MODIS/006/MOD13Q1 is from the Terra satellite and has the bands of Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) and Enhanced Vegetation Index (EVI) as vegetation layers. The region of interest (ROI) is south-western Germany covering a time period of 10 years from 1st January, 2007 to 31st December, 2017. We apply filters to the image collection. From literature review, different classification schemes exist for classifying biome (De Cáceres and Wiser, 2012). Within the ROI, different land surface covers are define to constitute biomes. Delineation of the biomes is undertaken with the support of the dataset of Map Legend in GEE. Next, we scale the image collection using “ .map(scale) ” function with the scaling factor 0.0001. Further, cloud masking function is created and applied to mask-out clouds. The band named “DetailedQA” conatins the Vegetation Index (VI) quality indicators and is used for defining bits for cloud. Finally, a time series chart is created and plotted.

Interpretation of NDVI time series (without cloud masking)

The result illustrates that while different biomes had different levels of maximum NDVI within the time series analyzed, the biomes appear to follow a common pattern of attaining the maximum and minimum NDVI within a common time frame. Cropland however deviates from this observation significantly upon visual inspection. Moreover, deciduous broadleaf forest persistently attained the highest maximum NDVI of approximately 0.9 around the month of June each year. This is closely followed by Mixed forest and Evergreen needleleaved forest. As expected, the NDVI of Urban builtup have been relatively low compared to other biomes in most periods of the time series. A more critical view indicates that while deciduous broadleaf forest, mixed forest, Evergreen needleleaved forest and urban builtup biomes are able to sustain their maximum NDVI in their respective cases for a longer period, cropland experiences a sharp decline immediately after attaining maximum NDVI hence a steep slope. A suggested explanation in this trend is that cropland is the most disturbed biome by human activity with persistent farming activities hence continuous change in land cover and land use compared to the other biomes. A plausible reason is that the maximum NDVI values of cropland are attained at periods of crop maturity after which there is immediate harvesting leading to a fast change in the land cover as reflected in the sharp drop in maximum NDVI.
