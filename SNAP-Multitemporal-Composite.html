Multi-temporal composite analysis with SNAP

This report demonstrates the use of remotely sensed images both radar and optical being Sentinel 1 and Sentinel 2 respectively in analyzing Normalized Difference Moisture Index (NDMI) and multi-temporal analysis. NDMI has many applications including crop water stress monitoring. The preprocessing and analysis tasks undertaken in this report were done with Sentinel Application Platform (SNAP) software and supported with QGIS for creating maps of the results. Images were acquired from Copernicus Open Access Hub. The report is structured to focus on capturing important metadata characteristics, workflow which includes image acquisition, image preprocessing, analysis, and presenting map output as well as interpreting them. For the purpose of clarity and ease of understanding, the general workflow is summarized in a diagram whiles details of the procedure is segmented to reflect the task being undertaken and documented a such. These are aimed at achieving the following main objectives (i) To calculate Normalized Difference Moisture index (NDMI) (ii) To estimate the change of NDMI (change detection) (iii) To create a SAR multi-temporal composite

Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) Multi-temporal composite analysis with SNAP

Multitemporal composite analysis involves combining images of different timestep to detect changes evident in a study area over time period under study. This analysis is quite simple and efficient in depicting both unchanged and change areas of phenomena being studied. In this tutorial, we undertake multi-temporal composite analysis with the SNAP tool. As part of the exercise, we will design a typical workflow for undertaking a multi-temporal composite analysis, view important metadata characteristics of the satellite image, image preprocessing, analysis, and presenting map output as well as interpreting them. So by the end of this tutorial, we will be achieving these objective;

  • Undertake image calibration, speckle filtering, terrain correction and image stacking
  • Undertake a SAR multi-temporal composite analysis

Image acquisition of Sentinel 1 images

We will be using radar images from Sentinel 1 for the analysis. We will use three images of different time steps of the same study area which will be the region of Mekong Delta. These images not only reflects different conditions of the study area at different times but also different land use activities in the area. In order to capture the phenomena we are analyzing very well, particualr attention will be paid to time period the image covers and for this we need some background knowledge of the study area. We will consider time periods where different land use activities are dominant. So, the selection of dates for the image will be based on the supposed periods for land preparation, planting and harvesting of rice in certain towns such as Mua. Clearly, these are activities will produce significant changes on the landscape that can be captured. We also have to take note that different towns undertake different activities at different times. The main phenomena that is being researched with the multitemporal composite analysis is land use changes depicting the growth stage of rice cultivation in the Mekong Delta region. With this understanding, we can now proceed to focus on the images to be used.

The image used for this analysis is a radar sensed image being sentinel 1 Ground Range Detector in Interferometric Wide (IW) swath mode. Three timesteps being 13th March 2018, 16th August 2018 and 7th January 2019 image covering the Mekong delta were download from Copernicus Access Hub. You can use these specifications to download the same images if you want have the same results that we have produced here. Preprocessing analysis of Sentinel 1 images

Preprocessing of the image began with image calibration which was done to convert pixel values of image to correspond with backscatter of the surface. For this exercise, the importance of calibration lies in comparing images of different times. Also, Speckle filtering was undertaken to improve image interpretation and visualization. Speckle is a “salt and pepper” effect inherent in radar images (Held et al, 2003). Further, terrain correction was undertaken to correct geometric distortions. These preprocessing analysis were repeated for all images of different timesteps. Prior to Multitemporal composite analysis of image, the preprocessed images were converted to decibels in logarithmic scale to improve visualization and stacked together.

Preprocessing of image

Now, we can start with the preprocessing of the images. This will be done with SNAP with instructions. We will process the first image and the rest of the images can process with the same method as a form of practice. All images have to be processed for the composite analysis. Let start the preprocessing with image calibration.

Procedure for image calibration

To undertake calibration, click on Radar button from the menu bar and navigate to Radiometric then click on Calibrate button. From the Calibration box that opened, select image to be calibrated in the Source product box. Click on Processing parameters button, and set the preferred processing parameter as Output sigma0 band. Click on Run button to execute calibration. We will notice that the surfix ‘Cal’ is added to the name of the product for saving in the Target product and can be rename as preferred. You can also follow the procedure which is visualized in the figure below.

Procedure for Speckle filtering

Speckle are salt grain-like noise on the image and has to be removed. To do so, we will click on Radar button from the menu bar and navigate to speckle filtering then click on Single Product speckle filter. From the dialog box of Single Product speckle filter, we have to set the appropriate Input / Output parameters. For Source product, we will select the calibrated image and provide a name for the Target Product. Now we have to choose the filter type that should be used. To do that, click on Processing Parameters button and select Lee as the preferred filter. Finally, click on Run button to execute speckle filtering. The workflow is visualized below

Source: Author

Procedure for Terrain correction

We will begin the terrain correction by clicking on Radar tool on the menu bar. Proceeding from that, we navigate to Geometric button followed by Terrain correction and then click on Range Doppler Terrain correction. From the dialog box of Range Doppler Terrain Correction, we will select the calibrated and speckle filtered image from the previous preprocessing result as the Source Product. Image stacking of preprocessed images

Now that all images for the analysis have been preprocessed, we have to stack them together into a single file. To stack images, click on Radar button on the menu bar, navigate to Coregisteration in the dialog box that opened and then to Stack Tools. Click on Create stack. In the Create Stack box, click on the plus sign to import all image files to be stack. The minus sign can be used to remove any file not to be stack. Click on Create stack button and select Product Geolocation for Initial Offset Method. Click on Run button to execute stacking. The workflow is illustrated in the diagram below for clarity.

Multitemporal composite analysis of stacked preprocessed images

We have completed all preprocessing analysis and are ready to the multitemporal composite analysis. Multitemporal composite analysis of the preprocessed images will be undertaken by combining the images in RGB band channels to detect changes that have occurred. This will produce a multitude of different colours indicating diverse changes mapped. Our aim is to detect changes in land use and we will not undertaken classification for now. The image shows the spatial distribution of the changes.

Multitemporal composite

To create SAR multitemporal composite, click on Window button on the menu bar, then navigate to and click Open RGB Image Window button. Now we need to carefully set the various images for the RGB channel box that opened. To do this, we will set the parameters of the various bands in the order of decibel of Sigma0 VH of first timestep image (2018-03-13) for the Red band channel, decibel of Sigma0 VH of second timestep image (2018-08-16) for the Green band channel and decibel of Sigma0 VH of third timestep image (2019-01-07) for the Blue band. Interpretation of Results

The multitemporal colour composite of images covering the time periods of 13th March 2018, 16th April 2018 and 7th January 2019 created depicts changes in the backscattering coefficients of land cover and land use such as rice cultivation in the region of Mekong delta. The time-steps indicates the rice growing seasons including harvesting which varies across the Mekong delta. Corresponding to the chosen dates, Mua which is a rice growing community in Mekong undertakes planting in August and harvesting in January (Liew et al, 1997). The map of multitemporal composite illustrates large variation in colour which corresponds to the large variety of rice cropping system, different growing season and variety of rice planted in the Mekong delta region as argued by Xuan (1975) and Liew et al, (1997). The grey areas indicate unchanged areas over the time covered with relatively constant backscattering coefficient. These areas are expected to be non-rice growing areas with relatively more permanent land cover during the timesteps considered. Such areas include inland melaleuca forests, the coastal mangrove forests where there is no significant change in the land cover compared to rice cultivation fields that have changed with farming activities within the short period studied. Grey areas which appear to be linear are linear settlements and roads which had relatively constant backscattering during the period (Liew et al, 1997).
